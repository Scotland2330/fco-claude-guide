# First Class Operations — Brand Guidelines

## Brand Positioning
Tagline: Built to Scale. Designed to Last.
Promise: We turn operational chaos into competitive advantage.
Audience: Ambitious founders and operators scaling £1M–£10M businesses

## Tone of Voice
Primary tone: Authoritative but approachable. Expert, not arrogant.
Secondary tone: Practical and action-oriented. We give people things they can use.

## Language Rules
DO use:
- "Operational clarity" not "getting organised"
- "Scale" not "grow" (growth is a by-product of scale)
- "Systems" not "processes" (systems imply automation)
- "Operators" not "managers" (operators execute, managers oversee)

DO NOT use:
- "Synergy", "leverage" (as a verb), "circle back", "touch base"
- Exclamation marks in formal content
- Passive voice ("it was decided" → "we decided")

# Workflow: Writing a Client Proposal

## Purpose
This workflow defines how FCO writes proposals for new retainer clients.
Follow this process exactly. Do not deviate.

## Step 1: Discovery Summary
Before writing, confirm you have:
- The client's primary pain point (in their words)
- Their current situation and what has failed before
- Their definition of success in 90 days
- Their budget range and decision timeline

## Step 2: Proposal Structure
Section 1: The Problem (mirror their language back to them)
Section 2: Why This Happens (show you understand the root cause)
Section 3: Our Approach (3-phase methodology, not a list of deliverables)
Section 4: What Success Looks Like (specific, measurable outcomes)
Section 5: Investment & Timeline
Section 6: Next Steps (one clear action, not multiple options)

## Rules
- Maximum 4 pages. Founders do not read long proposals.
- Lead with their problem, not our credentials.
- Never use the word "deliverables" — use "outcomes" instead.
- Every section should answer: "So what does this mean for me?"

Building Your Own Skills Files

A Skills file is a specialised context file that defines exactly how Claude should approach a specific type of task. Think of it as writing a job description for Claude — one that captures your methodology, your standards, and your non-negotiables.

💡
Why Skills Files Matter: A generic prompt produces generic output. A Skills file encodes your expertise — your frameworks, your quality standards, your decision rules — so Claude produces outputs that reflect your best thinking, not an average of the internet.

The Anatomy of a Skills File

📄 Skills File Template
# SKILL: [Name of the Skill]
# Version: 1.0
# Use When: [Describe the exact situation where Claude should apply this skill]

## PURPOSE
[One sentence: what does this skill help Claude do?]

## CONTEXT
[Background information Claude needs to do this well. 
Include relevant industry knowledge, your methodology, 
or any frameworks you use.]

## PROCESS
Step 1: [First action Claude should take]
Step 2: [Second action]
Step 3: [Continue as needed]

## QUALITY STANDARDS
- [Standard 1: e.g., "Every recommendation must include a specific metric"]
- [Standard 2: e.g., "Always present 3 options, not 1"]
- [Standard 3: e.g., "Flag any assumption you are making"]

## OUTPUT FORMAT
[Describe exactly how the output should be structured.
Headers, length, tone, format.]

## RULES
- [Non-negotiable rule 1]
- [Non-negotiable rule 2]
- NEVER [thing Claude must not do]
- ALWAYS [thing Claude must always do]

## EXAMPLES
[Paste 1–2 examples of ideal outputs for this skill]

🔥 Advanced Prompt

Turn Claude Into Your Brutal Critic

The most valuable use of Claude for operators is not writing content — it is stress-testing your thinking. This prompt turns Claude into the advisor who tells you what you need to hear, not what you want to hear.

Step 1

What Are You Actually Saying?

Claude reads between the lines. If you say "I'm thinking about hiring," it identifies whether you're making a strategic move or avoiding a harder conversation.

Step 2

Where Is Your Reasoning Broken?

Claude dissects your logic like a mechanic taking apart an engine — showing you the specific part that doesn't work and why.

Step 3

What Are You Avoiding?

Every time you dodge something hard, there's a cost. Claude calculates it and shows you what another week of avoidance actually costs your business.

Step 4

What Would an Expert Do Differently?

Not motivational — concrete. Claude shows you the specific gap between your current approach and what expert-level thinking looks like on the same problem.

Step 5

What Should You Actually Do?

A precise, prioritised action plan. What to start, what to stop, and what evidence would tell you it is not working.

Step 6

The Question You're Avoiding

Claude ends with the one uncomfortable question you need to sit with — the one that makes your stomach drop a little.

🔥 The Brutal Critic Prompt
You are my brutally honest thinking partner. Your job is to make 
my thinking sharper, my plans more realistic, and my blind spots 
visible — every single time we talk.

You are not my cheerleader. You are not my yes-man. You're the 
advisor who grabs my arm before I walk into a bad decision and 
says "Here's exactly why this is wrong."

For everything I share, do this:

1. What am I actually saying vs. what I think I'm saying?
2. Where is my reasoning broken? Show me the specific flaw.
3. What am I avoiding, and what is it costing me?
4. What would someone who's actually where I want to be do differently?
5. What should I actually do — in order, starting now?
6. What is the one question I'm clearly avoiding?

Rules:
- Never open with praise or agreement. Ever.
- Never soften a critique. Say the hard thing and let it land.
- No motivational clichés. Concrete language only.
- Keep it tight. A short, precise hit lands harder than a lecture.

I want to walk away from every conversation seeing something 
I couldn't see before — even if it stings.

📄 Step-by-Step Guide

How to Build Your MD & Skills Files From Scratch

A Markdown (.md) file is a plain text file with simple formatting. You do not need any special software. You need a text editor, 20 minutes, and the templates below. Here is the exact process for building every file type.

Step 1: Create the File

  1. Open Notepad (Windows), TextEdit (Mac, in plain text mode), or VS Code (recommended — free).
  2. Write your content using the templates below. Use # for headings, ## for sub-headings, and plain text for content.
  3. Save the file with a .md extension — e.g., about.md, brand.md.
  4. Create a dedicated folder on your computer called Claude Context and store all your .md files there.
  5. In Claude Cowork, point the folder selector at your Claude Context folder. Claude will read every file in it automatically.
💡
Or use the AI File Generator: Skip the blank page entirely. Our interactive agent asks you the right questions and builds your files automatically — ready to download and use.

Step 2: Name Your Files Correctly

Consistent naming makes it easy to reference files in prompts and keeps your folder organised as it grows.

File Name What It Contains
about.md Your role, goals, communication style, writing examples
brand.md Tone of voice, language rules, values, audience
workflow_[name].md Step-by-step process for a specific task type
skill_[name].md Domain expertise + quality standards for a task type
reference_data.md Pricing, metrics, product specs, key numbers
audience_profiles.md Client/customer profiles, pain points, goals

Step 3: What to Write in Each File

The quality of your output is directly proportional to the quality of your context files. Here is what to include in each one — and what most people miss.

👤

about.md — The Foundation

Most people write a job description. Write a brief instead — as if you're onboarding a new team member who needs to think like you.

  • Your role and what you're responsible for
  • What success looks like in your world
  • How you communicate (tone, format, pace)
  • Words/phrases you use and avoid
  • 2–3 examples of writing you're proud of
  • Your current top 3 priorities
🎨

brand.md — The Voice

This is the file that makes Claude sound like you, not like a generic AI. Be specific — vague instructions produce vague outputs.

  • Brand positioning in one sentence
  • Primary and secondary tone descriptors
  • Specific words you DO use (with examples)
  • Specific words you DO NOT use
  • Formatting rules (headers, bullets, length)
  • Target audience description
⚙️

skill_[name].md — The Expertise

This is where you encode your methodology. Think of it as writing the instructions for a highly capable contractor who knows nothing about your standards.

  • When to use this skill (trigger conditions)
  • Your step-by-step process
  • Quality standards and non-negotiables
  • Output format and structure
  • Rules: what to always do / never do
  • 1–2 examples of ideal output
